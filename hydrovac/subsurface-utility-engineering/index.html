The Process

What Subsurface Utility Engineering Is — and Where OE Fits

Subsurface utility engineering (SUE) is an engineering practice, not a piece of equipment. Defined by the ASCE 38 standard, it's the disciplined process an engineer uses to investigate, verify, and depict what's underground so a project can be designed and built without striking it. SUE sorts utility data into four quality levels: Quality Level D (QL-D) from existing records, Quality Level C (QL-C) from visible surface features, Quality Level B (QL-B) from surface geophysical methods, and Quality Level A (QL-A) — the highest — from physically exposing the utility and measuring it.

QL-A is where OE works. The only way to know exactly where a utility sits, and what condition it's in, is to expose it — and the standard calls for that exposure to be non-destructive. OE digs the test hole with hydrovac: high-pressure water and vacuum open a clean, controlled hole to the utility without striking it, the utility's true horizontal and vertical position is there to be surveyed, and its size, material, and condition can be recorded. The hole is backfilled and the data goes to the team building the SUE deliverable.

Where OE fits: SUE is certified by a professional engineer, and the quality-level designations, mapping, and conflict analysis are engineering deliverables. OE is the field-exposure partner in that process — the crew that performs the non-destructive QL-A test holes the engineer's program depends on. We provide verified field data; your engineer provides the SUE. Clear lanes, one reliable field partner.
Locates first: A QL-A test hole is itself an excavation next to a live utility. Before we open it, we work to confirmed locates — and the QL-B designation, where one exists, tells us where to expose. Non-destructive exposure is the right method for confirming a utility without harming it, but safer than the alternative is not the same as safe. The locate comes first.
